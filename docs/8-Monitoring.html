5  Monitoring

In this chapter, we introduce training monitoring. I will describe what it is, then give an overview of goals and overall principles before diving into specific methods used to monitor training.

Remember from Chapter 4 (once I write it down 😋) that monitoring is one of the two established strategies to handle the complexity of biological adaptations to training. Primarily, it is the observation of load and strain throughout training. The overarching goal is to adhere to the fundamental training principles1, avoid unwanted adaptations, minimize injury risk, and predict peak performance. These are high and hard-to-reach goals. In particular, reducing the risk of injury and improving the prediction of performance peaks are somewhat like the holy grails in sports science. Even for highly technological expert teams, these are very difficult to achieve in the complex reality.

Monitoring is the continuous observation and assessment of the training, and assists coaches and athletes in flexible decision-making throughout the process without dedicated testing. Specifically, we try to estimate external and internal loads and compare them to the prescribed training plan. This continuous observation is also a form of invisible testing. Instead of conducting dedicated tests, monitoring allows you to document the athlete’s performance or physiological response directly from training. Compared to explicit testing sessions, invisible testing has some pros and cons.

Briefly discussed: Explicit testing allows for a well-standardized procedure, leading to (hopefully) valid and reliable data that provide further insights into physiology or help differentiate constructs of performance. Think of laboratory tests like CPET or an isokinetic dynamometer to get precise values that you can not conduct easily on the field. They may be costly and time-intensive, need well-trained testers and preparation. Followingly, athletes (and coaches) might enter an explicit testing situation with quite a different mindset. The performance during a test might more closely reflect competition than training data. Explicit testing can also backfire if athletes or coaches manipulate the test to obtain lower test results. For example, at the beginning of the season, athletes may hold back a little, or the coach may hold them back or downplay the significance of the test. This makes sense if they want to present higher rates of improvement. It could also be a result of low buy-in, which comes back as your role as an effective communicator. Invisible testing, on the other hand, can give more realistic estimations of performance and adaptation. However, they lack standardization, and the data is generally messier. For more information on testing, see the upcoming chapter.)

Monitoring goes beyond tracking load parameters. The big advantage comes from monitoring markers of readiness, fatigue, health, behaviour, mental processes, physiological adaptations, and performance, thereby supporting auto-regulation. Auto-regulation refers to a flexible approach to load management or training content based on acute performance or psychophysiological states (Greig et al. 2020). For example, an athlete may measure their isometric grip strength daily to evaluate readiness and fatigue. Typically, their maximal force is 60 kg with a standard deviation (SD) of 3 kg. Whenever their grip strength drops under a certain cut-off (for example, $ 2 SD $), they consider reducing the training volume for the day by 20%, leaving out the most taxing exercise. Another example of autoregulation is adjusting the weight used during resistance exercises based on barbell velocity, or opting for a recovery day when morning HRV drops significantly.

Overall, there is good scientific evidence that autoregulation may be more effective than fixed prioritization for strength development (Zhang et al. 2021) and endurance training. Some recent studies show (surprisingly consistent) benefits for different physical attributes of speed skaters (Di, Hongye, and Donglin 2025). It is not clear which monitoring tools or parameters are most effective. Probably a combination of different objective and subjective metrics should be considered. A 2022 study of 20 amateur rugby players found greater improvements when implementing objective (velocity-based) auto-regulation than when using subjective (RPE-based) autoregulation for strength and power (Shattock and Tee 2022). However, this result may be very specific. Depending on the outcome of interest, some markers may be better than others. From a practical perspective, the choice is also always a question of resources and the athlete’s adherence.

We should also remember that, in scientific experiments, as in the studies presented, researchers typically aim to standardize the training process as much as possible to obtain a robust estimate of population-based results. In the context of auto-regulation, this means that the adaptation of training is typically fixed to a certain change in the monitoring variables. However, in practical settings, the monitoring variable is just a tool to start a conversation. Auto-regulation should be viewed as the flexible adjustment of training based on individual needs, established by the athlete, while the coach supports informed decision-making!
